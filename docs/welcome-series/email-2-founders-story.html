I want to tell you why this Institute exists — and it starts with a funeral.

In 2005, my wife Anna and I were studying at Oxford. We had grown up Mennonite in the Canadian prairies — Saskatchewan and British Columbia. I'd trained to be a Mennonite pastor, studied classics and philosophy, and spent years searching for the intellectual tradition that could hold together everything I believed about God, reason, and culture.

Then John Paul II died. We went to Rome for the funeral. And something happened there — in the crowds, in the liturgy, in the sheer weight of two thousand years of intellectual and spiritual tradition made visible — that completed a journey we'd been on for a long time. We entered the Catholic Church.

I spent the next two decades trying to build Catholic intellectual life in Canada. I helped establish three Catholic Studies programs. I served as Academic Dean at Newman Theological College in Edmonton, where we doubled enrollment and secured over a million dollars in outreach funding. I published eleven books on Catholic education and culture. I was honoured with the Queen Elizabeth II Platinum Jubilee Medal.

And through all of it, one thing became painfully clear: Canada has almost no institutional infrastructure for Catholic intellectual and cultural life. The United States has the Napa Institute, the Acton Institute, FOCUS, Word on Fire, the Augustine Institute, and dozens of others. Canada has almost nothing.

The Gregory the Great Institute exists to change that.

We are building an institution — not a project, not a program, but an institution — that will form Catholic leaders, publish serious scholarship, and cultivate the kind of community that can sustain cultural renewal over decades. We named it after St. Gregory the Great because Gregory did exactly this in the sixth century: he rebuilt civilization from the ruins of the Roman Empire through liturgy, education, and pastoral care.

In our first year, we published Magnalia — our journal of Catholic cultural renewal — and placed it in the hands of every Catholic bishop and seminary rector in Canada. We ran formation programs in four provinces. We hosted the inaugural Renewing Culture Conference. We built a community of over 1,500 people who believe, as we do, that Catholic intellectual life in Canada is worth rebuilding.

I'm glad you're here. The Magnalia Letter is one small part of this work — but an important one. The ideas that arrive in your inbox each month are drawn from the same tradition that changed our lives in Rome twenty years ago.

If anything in these emails resonates, I'd love to hear from you. Just reply — I read every response.

God bless,

Ryan
